Roommates

Fall 2020

A mobile app that streamlines the shared housing experience for college students living off-campus. Developed through Propel, an incubator program run by Cornell Design & Tech Initiative.

duration 3 months
type Incubator

Introduction

Roommates began as a mere idea between two roommates who worked and lived well together. Though our own living situation was unproblematic, we recognized through conversations with our peers a problem space that was unaccounted for: how to organize and communicate with your roommates. We wanted to design a product that would improve the shared housing experience for college students by addressing problems related to move-in, chores, expenses, and house rules.

Research

Our research consisted of market research and user research. This allowed us to understand our users as well as understand why there were no leading current solutions.

Research methods.

Market Research

We began by researching existing platforms that were similar to what we wanted to achieve. The current market has many applications that tackle a single aspect of living with others; for example, only chores, or only splitting expenses.

We discovered one app, RoomMate, that all-encompassed the features we had in mind, excluding how to track move-in information. We downloaded the app in order to gain a deeper look at the product and noted our key insights:

RoomMate

🡽

Manage multiple shared spaces with your friends, your family, your university classmates or whoever you prefer!

Key Insights:

  • The target audience is primarily European users.
  • The app has poor UI/UX (the interface is extremely cluttered, the user flow is not intuitive, the design is not appealing).
  • It takes too long to set up tasks, especially if recurring.
  • We stopped using it after one day.

Why did we end up not using an app that was so similar to what we wanted to accomplish?

Although the poor UX contributed to its failure, it became evident that the living problems we personally encountered were not painful enough to change the system we already had in place. After speaking with our mentors, we realized that in order for this product to be successful, it would have to be 10x better than anything that currently exists.

User Research

Understanding the biggest pain points experienced when living with someone else was crucial to designing a product users would actually commit to.

We released a survey that took an initial read of the challenges users faced with shared housing. The primary intention of this survey was to gauge what points were more painful than others — not quantify how painful they were.

Example survey results.

A preliminary analysis of our survey results made us realize that our current target audience of Cornell students was too broad. We decided to narrow the target audience of the MVP to only off-campus housings of 2–7 people.

We conducted 9 interviews with students within this user group in order to further understand what was making these living situations so stressful.

Interview remarks.

Insights

#1 College housemates/roommates struggle to manage and organize shared spaces.

  • This is primarily due to different cleanliness standards/lifestyles and a lack of communication, established systems, and accountability.
  • Situations are worse when living with strangers.

#2 Creating a system that everyone agrees on can be difficult.

  • Systems are in regards to chores, prorating rooms, and how to handle COVID-19.

#3 When housemates do set initial agreements, the system often fizzles out over time or only some stick to them.

  • Some chores or rules are not established in the beginning.
  • People avoid calling others out on things to eschew tension and confrontation.

#4 Current solutions are not centralized.

  • The application should be all-encompassing so that users do not have to switch between platforms, which makes it easier for users to commit and be held accountable.

Ideate

Based on our insights, we outlined user experience priorities:

UX priorities.

With the amount of time we had left before demo day, we also needed to prioritize some aspects of the design over others. We decided to prioritize breadth over depth, due to one of the problems being that existing solutions are not centralized.

We designed 5 key features of Roommates that addressed the insights we gained:

Onboarding — An individualized, anonymous survey allows everyone to express their preferences (Insight #2). A summary of the results are used to curate a suggested house rules list (Insight #1).

Home — The home page is split into a personal space and a shared space. The personal space is where the user can hold themselves accountable. The shared space is public to all housemates and contains the house rules as well as notifications, reminders, or comments intended for the whole house (Insight #1, Insight #3).

Expenses — The expenses page tracks all of the shared expenses (Insight #3). Users can only see who exactly has paid if they are the one requesting money.

Shopping — A standard, editable grocery list that allows "joining" specific items identifies which items are shared (ergo, which costs are split) (Insight #2).

Chores — When users first join the app, they must make a chores chart (Insight #1, Insight #2) where they add chores, assign days and frequencies, and then designate chores to different members. An anonymous pinging system enables users to ping the entire house if they noticed an incomplete chore overdue (Insight #3). Other notifications include indicating when you need to delay a chore or when you want to tell someone else they did a good job.

These 5 features together offer a centralized solution, as desired in Insight #4.

Takeaways

Understandably, the first iteration of our design has much to be improved upon. We did not allot ourselves enough time to both finish prototyping and begin user testing, and so a lot of our design was rushed. From this, however, I learned the importance of user testing, because there are many flaws that need to be fixed within our design, even though we had walked through it and fleshed it out in the ideation phase.

We created a solid foundation for future development — although the design is not perfect, my understanding of the problem space allowed me to create features I am proud of. I know that with more time, user research, and user testing, Roommates can truly make everyone’s home feel like home.

Gratefully, due to popular response from Demo Day, Roommates will be integrated as a Cornell Design & Tech Initiative project in 2021. I will be working as both a product manager and a UX designer to conduct more research and refine the design :-)
